WiDS Incubator Project: Outbrain Click Prediction¶

Alex Cho, Kenneth Lipke, Parisa Gandomkar

Overview¶

This project involved improving Outbrain’s recommendation algorithm by predicting which pieces of content its global base of users are likely to click on. Given a set of ads displayed on a web page, we were tasked to accurately rank the advertisements based on their likelihood of receiving a click.

The documents tables provide information about what is contained in the content of the documents on which the ads appear. For example, we have information on the website itself, e.g., are we looking at ads that appeared on CNN or a blog; what are the documents about, e.g., is it an article, is it an online marketplace; as well as metadata for the document, like the time it was published.

Unfortunately, these are all coded:

In our models, we focused on a few key pieces of information:

  • document category
  • publisher
  • document topic We ignored things like entity id, because after looking at the documentation for the data, this could be referring to anything from a person mentioned in an article, an organization, or even a location, so we figured this was more trouble than it would be worth. We also ignored published time.

Another piece of information that we did not take into account is confidence level. Outbrain wasn't actually sure of the information about each document, to drive this point home, they assign each observation a confidence level that indicates how sure they are of the topic, category, publisher, etc., that they are linking to a given document. We did not use this, though we mention in as a next step and will discuss it more later.

I was not expecting this. Going in, I figured that a small number of advertisers would be targeting a small number of users on specific platforms. However, what we are seeing here, is that there are a great number of publishers, but there aren't any huge players. The same is true for categories, and topics.

This informed the way we built the models that included documents. At first, the thought was, lets find out who the big players are, and include dummeis for those in our model. When there are no big players, though, we can't do that. This led to copmutational challeneges in dealing with all these different categories.
